In Week 3, I implemented feature engineering and exploratory modeling to bridge the gap between raw measurement variables and predictive signal for food-quality outcomes. Food processing datasets often require derived variables that capture meaningful relationships (e.g., ratios, cumulative exposures, and non-linear transformations) and reduce noise or skew in raw measurements. This week’s work focuses on creating such features in a controlled, explainable way and validating them with lightweight baseline models.

The pipeline first builds a binary quality-control target (“high quality”) from the original quality score using a clearly documented threshold rule: **high quality = quality ≥ 7**. On the cleaned dataset, this produces a **positive rate of 18.97%** (1,009 positives out of 5,320 rows), with segment differences that matter for later evaluation (approximately 13.54% for red and 20.83% for white). Next, it adds interpretable engineered features that reflect process-style reasoning: combined acidity, sulfur ratio (free vs total), log transforms for skewed variables, and interaction-style terms that approximate combined effects. The engineered dataset is saved to `data/processed/` to preserve transparency and enable later reuse.

For exploratory modeling, I trained two baseline models commonly used in industrial analytics: a regularized logistic regression (high interpretability) and a random forest (non-linear signal detection). I evaluated performance on a holdout test split and generated ROC/PR curves to assess ranking quality, including consideration for class imbalance. The executed results show the expected trade-offs: the random forest achieved **ROC-AUC 0.873** and **PR-AUC 0.626**, while the logistic regression achieved **ROC-AUC 0.819** and **PR-AUC 0.473** at the same operating threshold. Logistic regression delivered higher recall at the default threshold (more sensitive, lower precision), whereas the random forest delivered stronger ranking performance overall.

To ensure the work is not a “black box,” I exported the most influential logistic regression coefficients and the most important random forest features into CSV files. For example, the random forest ranked `density_alcohol`, `alcohol`, and `density` among the strongest drivers, while logistic regression highlighted strong negative association for `density` and `volatile_acidity`. These artifacts support feature validation and provide evidence-backed hypotheses about which measurements are associated with higher quality.

All scripts and outputs are organized week-wise for GitHub, providing evidence of executed feature engineering and exploratory modeling rather than only narrative documentation.
