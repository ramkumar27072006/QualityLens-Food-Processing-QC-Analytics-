In Week 1, I completed the foundational project work by selecting a real, publicly available food-quality dataset and implementing a reproducible data exploration workflow that generates concrete evidence (files/plots) for later modeling. I used the UCI Wine Quality dataset (red and white variants) as a practical stand-in for food processing quality metrics, because it provides measurable chemical properties and a quality score that can be treated as a quality-control outcome. The purpose of this week was not to jump into modeling prematurely, but to establish a rigorous exploration baseline and an analysis plan that can be extended and audited.

I created a week-wise script that downloads the dataset (if missing), loads and standardizes it, and then produces an EDA “artifact set” inside the Week 1 folder. The artifacts include an overview JSON summary (dataset size, column list, dtypes, missingness, duplicates, and the distribution of the quality label), descriptive statistics in CSV form, and multiple plots that support strategic planning: (1) the quality distribution (segmented by wine type), (2) a missingness overview to confirm data completeness assumptions, and (3) a correlation heatmap to highlight potential relationships and redundancy among variables.

The EDA results are based on the actual downloaded data: the combined dataset contains **6,497 rows** and **13 columns** (11 numeric chemistry variables plus `quality` and `wine_type`). It has **0 missing values**, but it does contain **1,177 exact duplicate rows**, which is a concrete data-quality issue to plan for (and which is handled explicitly in Week 2). The quality label distribution is also quantified (e.g., quality 6 is most common, followed by 5 and 7), which helps define realistic class balance expectations for later classification tasks.

To connect exploration to food-processing strategy, I summarized early signals that can drive downstream hypotheses and feature design. For example, in the cleaned dataset (Week 2 output), `alcohol` shows the strongest positive correlation with `quality` (**+0.469**), while `density` (**−0.326**) and `volatile_acidity` (**−0.265**) are among the strongest negative correlations. These results are treated as prioritization signals (not causal claims) and are used to guide what to validate, what to engineer into features, and what to test statistically in later weeks.

These outputs directly support food-processing analytics strategy: they help define the target and acceptable proxies, identify candidate drivers, and confirm whether the dataset is suitable for downstream cleaning rules, feature engineering design, and evaluation planning. This week’s work is fully reproducible and organized for GitHub publication.
