In Week 4, I extended the project from exploratory modeling into a more rigorous predictive modeling and hypothesis-testing stage. The purpose of this week is twofold: (1) to compare candidate predictive approaches using cross-validation so results are not dependent on a single split, and (2) to formalize evidence for potential drivers of food-quality outcomes via statistical tests and effect sizes.

First, I implemented a 5-fold stratified cross-validation evaluation for the baseline models introduced previously. This produces mean and variability estimates for ROC-AUC and PR-AUC, which are important in food-processing scenarios where “failure” or “high-risk” outcomes can be relatively rare. The executed results show that the random forest is the stronger ranker on this dataset (ROC-AUC **0.857 ± 0.014**, PR-AUC **0.588 ± 0.033**), while logistic regression is slightly lower but still competitive (ROC-AUC **0.832 ± 0.019**, PR-AUC **0.519 ± 0.029**). Cross-validation provides a stronger basis for deciding which modeling approach is most reliable before moving toward more advanced pipelines.

Second, I implemented numeric feature group comparisons between the two classes (high quality vs lower quality). For each numeric feature, I ran a Welch t-test (which does not assume equal variances) and computed an effect size (Cohen’s d) to capture practical significance. Because many features are tested simultaneously, I applied Benjamini–Hochberg false discovery rate control and reported adjusted p-values and rejection flags.

The resulting hypothesis table provides concrete, interpretable evidence for likely drivers. For example, `alcohol` shows a large and highly significant difference between classes (mean difference **+1.264**, Cohen’s d **+1.173**), while `density` is lower for high-quality samples (mean difference **−0.00223**, Cohen’s d **−0.787**). `volatile_acidity` is also lower for high-quality samples (mean difference **−0.0619**, Cohen’s d **−0.372**). These are treated as association signals that help prioritize which measurements should be monitored, engineered into features, or tested in more controlled studies.

The outputs are saved as structured CSV files inside the Week 4 folder so they can be reviewed, versioned, and published on GitHub. This week’s deliverables support the internship requirement of hypothesis formulation and testing methodology, while also producing real artifacts derived from an executed pipeline.
