## Week 1 — Data Exploration and Strategic Planning
In this Week 1 report, I developed a comprehensive exploration and strategic planning framework for analyzing trends and patterns in food processing quality metrics using publicly available data. The report starts by defining the project scope and success criteria in a way that generalizes to both batch/lot-level datasets (lab results, QA outcomes, yields) and time-series sensor datasets (temperature, pH, flow, conductivity, etc.). I then identified multiple credible open data sources relevant to food quality and safety—covering product attribute data, inspection/compliance signals, and well-documented academic datasets—and documented clear selection criteria (relevance, documentation quality, time coverage, licensing, and variable usefulness).

The report specifies an EDA methodology designed for food processing contexts, including baseline profiling (schema validation, missingness analysis, unit checks), univariate and bivariate exploration, segment comparisons (product line/facility/category), and time-based analyses where possible (rolling trends, seasonality concepts, change-point framing, and statistical process control-style stability checks). I also provided a realistic 30–35 hour work plan with milestones, resource allocation (tools, libraries, documentation practices), and a risk/mitigation section addressing common challenges such as unit inconsistency, missing timestamps, selection bias, and dataset mismatch versus factory data. Overall, the submission is intended to serve as the blueprint for the remaining weeks’ workflow.

## Week 2 — Data Cleaning and Preprocessing
In this Week 2 report, I designed a detailed data cleaning and preprocessing strategy specifically tailored to food processing datasets and sensor readings from production lines. The report begins by explaining why cleaning is critical in safety- and quality-sensitive environments, where sensor dropouts, mis-calibrations, and inconsistent recording practices can lead to incorrect conclusions. I then laid out a structured profiling plan to assess dataset structure (grain, keys, duplicates), statistical properties (distributions, skewness), and time-series integrity (sampling frequency, gaps, spikes, flatlines, and timestamp alignment).

The core of the report provides step-by-step cleaning techniques: a decision framework for missing values (classifying missingness causes, choosing imputation strategies by variable type, and adding audit flags), duplicate handling rules, and outlier/anomaly detection approaches that distinguish measurement errors from true process deviations. I included standardization guidance for units, categories, and timestamps, plus join-integrity checks to preserve traceability across batch, lab, QA, sensor, and maintenance tables. Finally, I documented preprocessing steps needed to create an analysis-ready dataset—scaling/normalization, categorical encoding, resampling and smoothing for time series, and leakage-preventing train/validation split strategies. The submission concludes with validation checks (schema, range, logic constraints) and a reproducibility plan (logs, versioning, and documented transformation rules).

## Week 3 — Feature Engineering and Exploratory Modeling
In this Week 3 report, I outlined a feature engineering strategy and an exploratory modeling approach for food processing analytics. The focus is on converting raw process data (batch metadata, lab results, and in-line sensor streams) into informative, stable, and interpretable features that can explain and predict quality outcomes. The report begins with principles and guardrails: each engineered feature must have a clear rationale, traceable lineage to raw inputs, and strict leakage prevention so that features only use information available prior to outcome labeling.

I described feature creation at two levels. For batch-level data, I proposed rates and ratios (defect rate, yield loss), compliance flags against specifications, and carefully controlled lag features. For sensor time series, I detailed time-window aggregation (mean/median, variability, percentiles, extremes), dynamics (slopes, rate-of-change), stability indicators (time above spec, threshold breach counts), and domain-inspired risk indices such as cumulative exposure above temperature thresholds. Where process phase markers exist (heating/holding/cooling/packaging), I emphasized phase-aware features to preserve the meaning of sensor behavior.

The report also includes an exploratory modeling plan to validate feature relevance: interpretable baselines (linear/logistic regression, shallow trees), non-linear exploratory models to detect interactions, and unsupervised methods (clustering/PCA/anomaly detection) when labels are weak. Finally, I proposed feature validation methods (importance, stability across segments, robustness to preprocessing choices) and documentation templates to ensure engineered features remain reliable for subsequent predictive strategy work.

## Week 4 — Predictive Modeling Strategy and Hypothesis Testing
In this Week 4 report, I developed a predictive modeling strategy and a hypothesis testing framework for a realistic food processing use case: predicting batch quality failure risk prior to release. The report clearly defines the predictive objective, target options (binary pass/fail or continuous quality score), and the decision point at which predictions must be made to support operational action without introducing label leakage.

I formulated multiple process-grounded hypotheses that connect measurable variables to outcomes, such as the impact of thermal control deviations (time-above-threshold), the interaction of pH and water activity on safety-related outcomes, the role of process instability (within-batch variability), supplier/product baseline differences, and the influence of cleaning/maintenance adherence. Each hypothesis is translated into concrete feature mappings so it can be tested in a repeatable way.

The modeling plan includes a justified algorithm shortlist: regularized logistic/linear regression as baseline models, tree-based methods for non-linear tabular process data, and time-series-aware approaches when sequence effects matter. I also defined evaluation metrics appropriate for rare, high-cost failures (PR-AUC, recall at fixed precision, calibration measures) and validation designs suited to manufacturing data (time-based splits and grouped validation by facility/line). Finally, the report outlines a complementary hypothesis testing toolkit—classical tests and regression-based inference with confounder controls, multiple testing corrections, and effect-size reporting—to ensure findings are statistically and practically meaningful.

## Week 5 — Model Evaluation Framework and Risk Assessment
In this Week 5 report, I created a robust model evaluation framework and a structured risk assessment plan for food processing analytics. The report explains why evaluation must be cost- and safety-aware: false negatives can have severe quality and safety implications, while false positives can generate unnecessary holds, waste, and downtime. To address this, I defined a layered evaluation approach that covers data validity, offline predictive performance, probability calibration, robustness across segments and shifts, and operational readiness.

The framework specifies a complete metric suite for both classification and regression problems (precision, recall, ROC-AUC, PR-AUC, MAE/RMSE), with additional emphasis on calibration metrics (Brier score, reliability curves) because risk scores must be trustworthy for decision-making. I outlined validation techniques that match real manufacturing constraints, including time-series splits, grouped validation by facility/line, bootstrap uncertainty estimation, and stress tests such as simulated sensor outages and noisy measurements.

A major component of the submission is risk assessment. I identified technical risks (overfitting, leakage, label noise), data risks (drift, calibration changes, shifting product mix), and operational/governance risks (misuse of scores, lack of monitoring, auditability gaps). I provided a practical risk register template and mitigation strategies including monitoring, retraining triggers, human-in-the-loop escalation rules, and conservative fallback behaviors when data is incomplete or model confidence is low. This report is designed to ensure any future model is reliable, auditable, and safe to use.
